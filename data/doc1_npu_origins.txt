Title: Origins and Early History of Neural Processing Units

The concept of a Neural Processing Unit (NPU) emerged from decades of research in artificial intelligence hardware acceleration. Unlike general-purpose CPUs or even graphics-focused GPUs, NPUs are purpose-built silicon designed specifically to accelerate the matrix multiplications and activation functions that underpin neural network inference and training.

The earliest dedicated neural network hardware can be traced to the late 1980s and early 1990s, when academic labs began experimenting with analog VLSI circuits that mimicked synaptic weights. Carver Mead at Caltech pioneered neuromorphic engineering, a term he coined in 1990, which described circuits that implemented biological neural computations directly in silicon. These early devices were not programmable in the modern sense but demonstrated that dedicated hardware could dramatically outperform software running on general CPUs.

The first commercially significant wave of neural accelerators appeared in the 1990s with chips like the Intel 80170NX Electrically Trainable Analog Neural Network (ETANN), released in 1989. ETANN featured 64 neurons and 10,240 programmable synaptic weights implemented as floating-gate transistors. It could perform 2 billion multiply-accumulate operations per second, a staggering figure for its era. However, limited programmability and the dominance of software-based neural networks during the AI winter of the 1990s meant commercial adoption was minimal.

The modern NPU era began in earnest after the deep learning revolution of 2012, when AlexNet demonstrated that large convolutional neural networks trained on GPUs could achieve superhuman image recognition accuracy. This result triggered an arms race in AI hardware. Google was among the first major companies to respond with dedicated silicon, beginning the design of its Tensor Processing Unit (TPU) in 2013. The TPU v1 was deployed internally in Google data centers in 2015 and revealed publicly in 2016. It was designed exclusively for inference, using 8-bit integer arithmetic to maximize throughput per watt.

Simultaneously, smartphone manufacturers began integrating neural accelerators into mobile System-on-Chip (SoC) designs. Apple introduced the first widely publicized on-device NPU with the A11 Bionic chip in 2017, which it branded the "Neural Engine." This 2-core unit could perform 600 billion operations per second and was used for Face ID facial recognition and augmented reality applications. The A11 Neural Engine marked a turning point: NPUs moved from data centers into the pockets of hundreds of millions of consumers.

Qualcomm, MediaTek, and Samsung quickly followed with their own Hexagon DSP-based NPU designs and Dimensity AI Processing Units. By 2019, virtually every flagship mobile SoC included a dedicated neural accelerator. The terminology itself became an industry battleground, with companies using terms such as NPU, Neural Engine, AI Engine, and APU interchangeably to describe what are functionally similar architectures.
