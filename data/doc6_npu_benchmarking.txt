Title: NPU Performance Benchmarking and Evaluation Standards

Comparing NPU performance across vendors is notoriously difficult. Raw TOPS (Tera Operations Per Second) figures are frequently misleading: they reflect peak theoretical throughput under ideal conditions — typically dense INT8 matrix multiplication — which rarely corresponds to real-world workload performance. Understanding NPU benchmarking requires familiarity with both the metrics and their limitations.

The MLCommons organization, a consortium of AI companies and researchers, has developed the MLPerf benchmark suite as the most widely adopted standardized NPU evaluation framework. MLPerf consists of two divisions: a Closed Division with fixed models and strict implementation rules for apples-to-apples comparison, and an Open Division that allows architectural innovation. Key MLPerf inference benchmarks include image classification (ResNet-50), object detection (RetinaNet), medical image segmentation (3D U-Net), natural language processing (BERT-Large), large language model generation (GPT-J and Llama 2), and recommendation (DLRM-v2). Results are reported as queries per second (QPS) for throughput scenarios and as 99th-percentile latency for latency-constrained scenarios.

TOPS figures are problematic for several reasons. First, different vendors use different precision levels: 45 TOPS at INT4 is not directly comparable to 35 TOPS at INT8 because INT4 is twice as dense but may have lower accuracy for some models. Second, peak TOPS assume 100% hardware utilization, but real models have varying operator mixes, memory-bound phases, and irregular computation graphs that prevent full utilization. Third, system-level bottlenecks — PCIe bandwidth, DRAM speed, CPU pre/post-processing — can dominate end-to-end latency even when the NPU itself is fast.

Roofline analysis is a more principled approach to NPU performance evaluation. The roofline model plots achievable performance (FLOPS/second) as a function of arithmetic intensity (FLOPS per byte of memory traffic). Operations with high arithmetic intensity (dense matrix multiplications) are compute-bound and can approach peak TOPS. Operations with low arithmetic intensity (embedding lookups, normalization layers) are memory-bandwidth-bound and are capped by memory bandwidth, not compute. By measuring where different operations fall on the roofline, architects can identify whether hardware investment should prioritize more compute or more memory bandwidth.

Energy efficiency benchmarking is increasingly important as electricity costs become a primary constraint for both data centers and edge deployments. The TOPS-per-watt metric captures efficiency but shares the same limitations as raw TOPS. MLPerf Power is an extension of the MLPerf benchmark that measures power consumption during inference runs, enabling legitimate energy efficiency comparisons. Google reports its TPU v4 achieves approximately 200 TOPS/watt at INT8, while Apple's M-series Neural Engine achieves over 100 TOPS/watt in the mobile segment.

The AI model efficiency benchmarking landscape also includes vendor-specific tools. Apple's Core ML Performance Report measures inference latency on specific devices. Qualcomm's AI Model Efficiency Toolkit (AIMET) provides quantization accuracy evaluation alongside performance profiling. These tools are essential for developers deploying models on specific hardware but do not enable cross-vendor comparisons.

Emerging benchmarking challenges include evaluating long-context transformer inference, where KV cache memory management dominates performance, and mixture-of-experts (MoE) model inference, where sparse expert routing creates irregular, hard-to-predict computation patterns. The MLPerf roadmap includes benchmarks targeting these workloads, reflecting the field's rapid evolution toward ever-larger and more complex AI models.
