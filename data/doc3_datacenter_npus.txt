Title: NPUs in Data Centers and Cloud AI Infrastructure

The data center NPU market has become one of the most competitive and strategically important segments of the semiconductor industry. Unlike mobile NPUs constrained by thermal design power (TDP) budgets of 1-5 watts, data center NPUs can consume hundreds of watts and are optimized for raw throughput and the ability to run the world's largest AI models.

Google's Tensor Processing Unit family represents the longest-running lineage of data center NPUs. The TPU v1 (2016) was inference-only with 92 TOPS at INT8. The TPU v2 (2017) added training support with 180 teraflops of bfloat16 performance and introduced high-bandwidth memory (HBM). The TPU v3 (2018) doubled compute to 420 teraflops and added liquid cooling to handle its 250-watt TDP. The TPU v4 (2021) reached 275 teraflops of bfloat16 and introduced the Inter-chip Interconnect (ICI), a high-speed optical network linking 4,096 TPU chips into a single logical accelerator called a TPU pod. The TPU v5e and v5p variants followed in 2023 with further efficiency improvements targeting cost-sensitive inference workloads at scale.

Amazon Web Services developed its own NPU lineage under the Inferentia and Trainium brands. AWS Inferentia (2019) was designed for low-cost, high-throughput inference and featured 128 GB/s of memory bandwidth with support for FP16, BF16, and INT8. The Inferentia2 (2023) dramatically improved on this with 2.3x higher throughput and 4.5x lower latency than its predecessor. AWS Trainium (2021) targeted training workloads with 440 teraflops of BF16 performance. These chips underpin Amazon's EC2 Trn1 and Inf2 instance types, giving cloud customers NPU access without owning hardware.

Microsoft partnered with AMD and developed the Maia 100 AI Accelerator, announced in 2023, targeting Azure's internal AI workloads including inference for large language models powering Copilot services. The Maia 100 is manufactured on TSMC's 5nm process and is integrated into custom rack designs that optimize cooling and power delivery for AI density.

Meta developed the Meta Training and Inference Accelerator (MTIA) announced in 2023, focused on ranking and recommendation workloads that dominate Meta's infrastructure costs. Unlike transformer-focused NPUs, MTIA is optimized for sparse embedding lookups and irregular memory access patterns characteristic of recommendation systems. The MTIA v1 achieves 102.4 TOPS at INT8 with 128 GB/s memory bandwidth.

Interconnect technology is as important as the NPU chip itself at data center scale. NVIDIA's NVLink, Google's ICI, and Cerebras's on-wafer interconnect each attempt to solve the same problem: making thousands of accelerators behave as one giant compute engine. Cerebras takes the most radical approach with its Wafer Scale Engine (WSE), a single die the size of an entire silicon wafer containing 900,000 AI cores connected by an on-wafer fabric with 220 petabytes per second of bandwidth, eliminating the chip-to-chip communication bottleneck entirely.

The energy efficiency of data center NPUs has become a primary competitive dimension as hyperscalers face electricity constraints. Google reports that its TPU v4 pods achieve 1.7x better performance per watt than comparable GPU-based systems for training large language models. Efficiency is measured in TOPS per watt, and the race to improve this figure is driving research into new memory technologies, advanced packaging like chiplets and 3D stacking, and novel dataflow architectures.
